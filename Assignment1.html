Conclusions from Part 2:

Comparing the descriptive statistics of the age variable for the customers who purchased a PEP versus customers who did not purchase a PEP, it is evident that older customers in general tended to be more likely to purchase a PEP. That is, the median age for customers who purchased a PEP was 45 years, while the median age for customers who did not purchase a PEP was only 40 years. Moreover, the interquartile range (IQR) for customers who purchased a PEP was 34-58 years of age, while the interquartile range (IQR) for customers who did not purchase a PEP was 27-51 years of age. As far as income is concerned, customers who purchased a PEP tended to have higher incomes than customers who did not purchase a PEP. The median income for customers who purchased a PEP was 28,080.05, while the median income for customers who did not purchase a PEP was only 23,105. Likewise, the IQR of income for customers who purchased a PEP was 19,639.48 to 38,458.35, and the IQR of income for customers who did not purchase a PEP was only 15,366.48 to 31,210.90. With respect to the number of children, the average number of children for customers who purchased a PEP (.9453) was slightly lower than the average number of children for customers who did not purchase a PEP (1.0675), but not dramatically different.

Since the distribution of the gender variable was evenly divided across the initial set of data (with 50% male customers and 50% female customers), this attribute was relatively easy to interpret. That is, a slightly higher percentage of males purchased a PEP (~ 53%), compared to the percentage of females who purchased a PEP (~ 47%). Customers who live in suburban areas were slightly more likely to purchase a PEP, while customers who live in towns were slightly less likely to purchase a PEP. However, there was not much information to be gained from the other two categories of the region variable; the distribution percentages of customers who live in the inner city or rural regions and either purchased a PEP or did not purchase a PEP aligned very closely with the initial distribution percentages of the complete/full dataset for these two categories of the region variable. As far as the married categorical variable is concerned, unmarried customers tended to be more likely to purchase a PEP compared to married customers. That is, compared to the distribution percentages of unmarried customers (35%) versus married customers (65%) in the initial/complete dataset, a larger percentage of customers who purchased a PEP were unmarried (~ 44%) and a smaller percentage of customers who purchased a PEP were married (~ 56%).

There was not much information to be gained from the car variable, as the distribution percentages of customers who purchased a PEP and customers who did not purchase a PEP closely aligned to that of the distribution percentages of the complete/total dataset (< 2% difference). For the variable savings account, people who did not have a savings account tended to be slightly more likely to purchase a PEP compared to customers who had a savings account.
